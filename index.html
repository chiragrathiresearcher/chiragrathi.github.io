Who I Am

Researcher based in Delhi, India. Research programme spans three domains: (i) theoretical cosmology, focusing on nonlinear structure formation and void dynamics within the ΛCDM framework; (ii) safety-critical autonomous systems for aerospace applications; and (iii) formal verification methodologies for real-time decision architectures.

Two manuscripts are currently under peer review at Foundations of Physics (Springer Nature). Open-source software systems — ARVS, ATLAS, and M17 — developed to production-grade standards, with formal specifications, documented validation protocols, and DOI-citable releases via Zenodo under Apache 2.0.

In 2026, NEXERCH was established as an independent research organization to consolidate development and dissemination of advanced autonomous systems. Incoming BS-MS candidate at IISER Pune, programme in Astrophysics and Theoretical Physics.

SYS — 001
ARVS
Adaptive Robust Verification System
Formal autonomy architecture addressing the problem of unbypassable safety constraint enforcement in spacecraft, rovers, and safety-critical cyber-physical systems. Implements 25 axiomatically frozen constraints across 9 operational categories within a non-bypassable authority layer. Empirically validated against live telemetry from NASA REMS (Perseverance), JPL SPICE/HORIZONS, ESA Mars Express, and ISS OSDR. Decision model grounded in MDP/POMDP formalism with A* mission planning and a 69-test deterministic verification suite.

SYS — 002
ATLAS
Autonomous Launch & Termination Architecture System
Autonomous guidance, navigation, and control architecture for reusable launch vehicles, addressing real-time state estimation and fault-tolerant actuation under hard timing constraints. Core loop operates at 1 kHz in C++17 with RTOS compatibility. State estimation via a 19-state Extended Kalman Filter with triple-redundant IMU voting for fault isolation. All hardware parameters derived directly from manufacturer datasheets, ensuring physical fidelity of the simulation environment.

SYS — 003
M17
K-Framework for Autonomous Celestial Discovery
Computational pipeline for autonomous celestial object detection and deep-space forensic analysis, operating within a multi-language heterogeneous architecture (C++17, Fortran, Rust, CUDA). Incorporates post-Newtonian relativistic corrections to orbital mechanics and implements heuristic methods for dark matter signature detection in photometric and astrometric datasets. Designed for space domain awareness at scale with GPU-accelerated processing via CUDA kernels.
